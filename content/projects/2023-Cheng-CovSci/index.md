**Abstract**
Scientific collaboration catalyzes knowledge diffusion and breeds innovation. However, the COVID-19 pandemic has disrupted the rhythm of research cooperation. To track the dynamics and key drivers of scientific collaboration after the COVID-19 outbreak, we analyzed a scientific collaboration network from 25.2 million publications worldwide from 2010 to 2022. We found that the volume of non-COVID-related international collaboration declined 22.2% by late 2022 compared to the pre-pandemic growth trend, with greater impacts on social science, large teams, and top schools. Comparatively, COVID-related international collaboration increased by 179.7%. The scholar-level analysis revealed that researchers developed fewer new collaborators, shifted their research interests to COVID-related topics, and junior academics were more affected. Despite remarkable changes, the impact of the pandemic on international collaborations is less conspicuous than on domestic collaborations. Our findings suggest that funders and institutes should balance rapid funding for disciplines and scientists being most disrupted to recover from the pandemic.
